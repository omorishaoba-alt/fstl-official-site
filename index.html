What blocks housing, infrastructure and investment in Nigeria?

  • Title uncertainty: fragmented records, unresolved encumbrances and disputed titles discourage lenders and investors.
  • Low mortgage penetration: formal mortgage markets remain shallow — mortgage penetration is under 1% of GDP.
  • Weak project finance channels: heavy reliance on foreign borrowing creates FX risk and conditionality.
  • Public system constraints: FHA and FMBN are constrained by legacy roles, limited capitalisation and process inefficiencies.

FHA & FMBN — what should change

Observed constraints

  • FMBN: conceptually a secondary market vehicle but historically undercapitalised and operationally spread across multiple roles which limit scale.
  • FHA: mandates for housing delivery compete with the need for standardised land packages and low-income housing focus — execution has been inconsistent.

Recommended model (Fannie/Ginnie adapted)

Split responsibilities to increase efficiency:

  • FMBN → Secondary market & liquidity engine: buy mortgages from originators, structure MBS/ABS, provide yield curves and investor-ready bonds.
  • FHA → Low-income housing authority: standardise land parcels, manage subsidised delivery and coordinate social housing programmes.

FSTL’s operational role

FSTL provides verified collateral, title guarantees and mortgage insurance that make the FMBN model bankable under local conditions.

Municipal & Infrastructure Bonds — a practical alternative

Advantages over foreign borrowing:

  • Local currency funding removes FX repayment risk.
  • Project-linked transparency ties funds to specific infrastructure outcomes.
  • Domestic investor base (pension funds, insurers, banks) keeps returns and economic benefit within Nigeria.
  • Complementary to MBS/ABS where verified property portfolios enhance bond credit quality.

Key statistics (source links recommended)

  • Estimated national housing deficit: ~17,000,000 units (industry reports 2024–2025).
  • Required annual build rate: ~550,000 units/year to significantly reduce the deficit.
  • Mortgage penetration: under 1% of GDP — formal mortgage market remains shallow.
  • Urbanisation: accelerating demand for serviced land and housing.
