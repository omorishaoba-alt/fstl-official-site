Legal Precedents

Key cases illustrating the importance of secure land rights and title insurance:

  • Ogunleye v. Oni: Reinforces the need for proper land registration; delays in court increase risk for buyers and hinder investments.
  • Olumide v. Adeyemi: Demonstrates the importance of accurate land documentation for protecting third-party interests and reducing fraud.
  • Okoro v. Uba: Confirms that Certificates of Occupancy (C of O) do not guarantee absolute ownership; highlights the necessity of title insurance.
  • Adebayo v. Ogun: Establishes statutory time limits for property claims, showing how time and cost constraints impede property rights without insurance.
  • Emmanuel v. Nwosu: Clarifies the limits of government-issued documents in guaranteeing land rights; reinforces title insurance as essential risk mitigation.

Title Guarantee & Insurance

Title insurance ensures legal recognition of ownership and protection against disputes, fraud, and defects:

  • Enables mortgage collateralisation and capital market participation.
  • Supports probate and intergenerational wealth planning.
  • Reduces litigation costs and delays.
  • Secures property investment confidence for individuals, companies, and government stakeholders.

Key Insight: Government-issued Certificates of Occupancy do not guarantee ownership; title insurance addresses this critical gap.

Governance & Risk

Federal housing institutions (FHA/FMBN) deficiencies and implications:

  • Poor risk management and capital allocation, reducing housing availability.
  • Weak enforcement of mortgage insurance, limiting investor confidence.
  • Lack of securitisation infrastructure compared with Fannie Mae/Ginnie Mae.
  • Fragmented oversight creates inefficiencies and limits scalability.

PMI/FHA/FMBN Analysis

Primary Mortgage Institutions (PMIs) provide housing finance but face significant deficiencies:

  • Poor risk assessment and management, limiting lending capacity.
  • Low rural coverage and weak regulatory enforcement.
  • FHA: Weak mortgage enforcement and undercapitalisation.
  • FMBN: Fragmented oversight and poor private sector integration.
  • Comparison with international counterparts (Fannie Mae/Ginnie Mae): Nigerian institutions lag in securitisation, risk standardisation, and investor confidence.
